Rachael's Sourdough Bread

Sourdough is a high hydration bread that rises through fermented yeast. As the bread ferments, healhty bacteria cause the dough to rise. These bacteria support the microbiome, healing the lining of the gut. Sourdough has helped me overcome my food sensitivities, so I love helping others learn how to heal their own body through food.

Ingredients:

1000 grams unbleached flour
330 grams of active sourdough starter
650 grams of water
25 grams of salt

Materials:

Cast Iron Pot
Bread Lame
Food Scale
Proofing Baskets

Instructions:

Measure your active, bubbly starter and water into a large mixing bowl. Mix until combined. Add 1000 grams of flour to the mixture and combine using your hands. (The dough may require a bit of extra water to help everything combine.) When combined, the dough should have a “shaggy” texture. Cover with a tea towel and allow to rise for 1.5-2 hours. (The longer the better!)

After your dough has risen for 1.5-2 hours, add salt to your dough. Slightly wet your fingertips, and combine the salt into the dough. After the salt is combined, cover the dough and allow it to rise for 2 hours. The dough can also take this rise in the fridge overnight.

After the second rise, flip the dough onto a flat surface. Spread the dough into a thin layer. Fold the dough over itself, roll into a ball, and shape the ball into a loaf. Create tension in the loaf by pushing the dough away from you, tucking the dough under itself, and pulling it towards you. Allow to rest on the counter for 20 minutes. Reshape after 20 minutes, place in floured proofing baskets, and allow to set in the fridge for 1 hour.

Preheat your oven to 500 degrees. Place your cast oven pot into the oven as the oven is preheating. Take your bread lame and score a design into the top of the loaf.

Set the loaf onto parchment paper, and place into the cast iron. Cover with the lid, and allow to bake for 30 minutes.

After 30 minutes, remove the lid from the pot and allow to bake for 20 more minutes until the loaf crisps. When baking is completed, let the loaf rest until cooled. Cut, and enjoy with fresh butter and jam!

Sourdough Starter

Making sourdough starter is a simple process that requires only two ingredients: flour and water.

Here's a basic recipe to make a sourdough starter:

Ingredients:

1 cup all-purpose flour
1/2 to 3/4 cup filtered water

Instructions:

Mix the flour and water together in a glass jar or a bowl. The consistency should be similar to thick pancake batter.

Cover the jar or bowl with a clean cloth or a kitchen towel. Leave it in a warm place, ideally between 70 to 75°F, for 24 hours.

The next day, check on the mixture. You should see some bubbles and notice a slightly sour smell. This means that the wild yeast and bacteria are starting to develop. Discard about half of the mixture and feed the remaining starter with equal parts of flour and water (e.g., 1/2 cup of each). Mix well, cover, and leave it again in a warm place for another 24 hours.

Repeat the feeding process every day, discarding half of the mixture and adding equal parts of flour and water until the starter is ready to use. The starter should be ready in about 7 to 10 days, depending on the temperature and humidity of your environment. Once the starter is active and bubbly, you can store it in the fridge and feed it once a week or so. To use the starter in a recipe, take it out of the fridge, let it come to room temperature, and feed it with fresh flour and water before using.

Note: The first few days of making a sourdough starter can be unpredictable, so don't worry if it doesn't look or smell exactly right. Just keep feeding it, and it will eventually become active and healthy.

Materials

For this recipe, you will need unbleached flour, water, your sourdough starter, and salt. Other materials needed to prepare the loaves are proofing baskets, a food scale, a cast iron lidded pot, and a bread lame (or clean razor blade).

The First Rise

Measure 330 grams of your active, bubbly starter and 650 grams of water into a large mixing bowl. Mix until combined. Add 1000 grams of flour to the mixture and combine using your hands. The dough may require a bit of extra water to help everything combine. When combined, the dough should have a “shaggy” texture. Cover with a tea towel and allow to rise for 1.5-2 hours. (The longer the better!) This first rise is called an autolyse.

The Second Rise

After your dough has risen for 1.5-2 hours, add 25 grams of salt to your dough. Slightly wet your fingertips, and combine the salt into the dough. Adding salt before this point causes the bread not to rise as easily. By waiting to add the salt at this point (after the autolyse), we are allowing our bread to rise the most it can. After the salt is combined, cover the dough and allow it to rise for 2 hours. The dough can also take this rise in the fridge overnight.

Shaping Your Dough

After the second rise, flip the dough onto a flat surface. Spread the dough into a thin layer. Fold the dough over itself, roll into a ball, and shape the ball into a loaf. Create tension in the loaf by pushing the dough away from you, tucking the dough under itself, and pulling it towards you. See the video on how to shape sourdough. Allow to rest on the counter for 20 minutes. Reshape after 20 minutes, place in floured proofing baskets, and allow to set in the fridge for 1 hour.

Design Loaf

Preheat your oven to 500 degrees. Place your cast iron pot into the oven as the oven is preheating. This will help your cast iron create steam in the pot and help the bread rise more. Next, take your bread lame, score a design into the top of the loaf.

Bake Loaf

Set the loaf onto parchment paper, and place into the cast iron. Cover with the lid, and allow to bake for 30 minutes. After 30 minutes, remove the lid from the pot and allow to bake for 20 more minutes until the loaf crisps. After the baking is completed, let the loaf rest until cooled. Cut, and enjoy with fresh butter and jam! .
